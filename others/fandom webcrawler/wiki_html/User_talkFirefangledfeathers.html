Poetry

[edit]

Just in case you take an interest in such matters – something about the Wallace Stevens poem you mention on your user page reminded me of a specific tradition of symbolism. A quick Google search showed I wasn't the first to make that connection: [15] Anyway, I love that poem. Regards, Andreas JN466 22:27, 15 November 2024 (UTC)[reply]

Interesting! Was it a particular line or phrase that reminded you of Sufi symbolism? Firefangledfeathers (talk / contribs) 02:17, 16 November 2024 (UTC)[reply]
The poem is really replete with such phrases, which is why I was so struck by it. (I had never heard of Stevens before and have not read anything else by him.) The poem starts with the palm, a symbol of baraka ("a kind of continuity of spiritual presence and revelation") in Sufi mysticism (cf. also Berakhah in Judaism). For an explanation see Shah: "The palm tree, as noted elsewhere, is the Sufi term for baraka ... an allegory of a Sufi process, later associated with the alchemical idea of the 'red elixir'." Note also Shah's comments on tariqa here – the palm may symbolise both an inner experience in Sufi mysticism and a specific Sufi teaching, or Sufi order. (Sufi poetry is generally known for choosing and striving for words that invite the reader to test and unfold a whole variety of different associations and meanings; see īhām.) "The end of mind", "beyond the last thought" is suggestive of a spiritual endeavour, seeking the "peace that passeth understanding", rising above the world of "bronze" (a non-noble metal). Next comes a "gold-feathered bird". Why gold? The concept of turning base metal (the ordinary human) into gold is familiar from Western alchemy. The step from the first noble metal, silver (moon), to gold (sun), involves adding a tinge of red. The quote mentioned above already alludes to the concept of reddening in Western alchemy, which came to the Christian world via the Sufi Jabir ibn Hayyan, known in Latin as Geber. "Without human feeling"; the Sufis speak of "being in the world but not of it" and of "dying before you die", which implies breaking identification with the events of daily life even while fully involved in it. Life is then experienced differently, "a foreign song". "The palm stands on the edge of space" – cf. the Flammarion engraving. Lastly, "the bird's fire-fangled feathers dangle down", providing a bridge between the ordinary and the transcendent experience of life. "Fire" is the main tool of alchemy, symbolising something akin to Buddhist mindfulness, a dual attention that has an inward and an outward aspect, i.e. remembrance of self, of breath (self and breath are the same words in Greek, Arabic and Hebrew) while being engaged in life – i.e. not being "heedless". So the fire-fangled feathers hanging down are the bridge between the lower and the higher, the outer and the inner. Birds symbolising inner experience are common both in Sufism (cf. Attar's The Conference of the Birds) and Christianity (cf. dove as the Holy Ghost etc.); and when reading Stevens' "Mere Being" (what an understatement!) I was also reminded of a charming poem by Rumi that has birds in it and which seemed to be made of the same cloth: [16] (the linked page has a number of different translations of the poem, of varying quality, but each has something to commend it). Regards, Andreas JN466 00:03, 17 November 2024 (UTC)[reply]
